Near-Field Wireless Power Transfer for 6G Internet of Everything Mobile Networks: Opportunities and Challenges

The paper provides an overview of the opportunities and challenges that arise from radiating near-field WPT. With the transition of 6G systems to mmWave frequencies combined with the use of large-scale antennas, future WPT devices are likely to operate in the radiating near-field.

A limitation of charging wireless devices using NFC WPT is given by the very small distance required between the power source and the charging devices.

6G wireless technologies will utilize large scale antenna arrays to communicate with user devices at the mmWave and sub-THz bands. Therefore, 6G-based IoE devices are expected to transmit and receive EM signals while operating in the radiative near-field.

In such regimes, devices located at distances ranging from a few centimeters to several tens of meters reside in the radiating near-field regime

The fact that IoE devices are likely to receive signals in the near- field regime combined with the potential beam focusing capabilities arising in such domains moti- vate the exploration of radiating near-field WPT for 6G IoE networks.

The expected operation of 6G networks, which will utilize large antenna arrays and high-frequency signals, gives rise to several implications for radiating WPT systems. The short wavelengths of high-frequency signals facilitates packing a large number of antenna elements into a given area, forming a large antenna array of limited physical aperture.

The antenna gain stemming from using large antenna arrays has the potential of compensating for the attenuation of high-frequency signal propagation over distance. The paper demonstrates that despite the WPT for NFC requires a short distance between transmitter and receiver, there are techniques that allow to compensate this issue.

Given that the 6G networks are going to use arrays of antennas, the paper shows how to take advantage of this for increasing the distance (using the beam steering) in which a transmitter can transfer power to receivers using NFC.

 

NFC Sensors Based on Energy Harvesting for IoT Applications

This paper describes how to build a NFC sensors battery less sensors system. The system is powered by harvesting energy from a the electromagnetic induction between 2 loops antennas.

There is a comparison of RFID technologies, which helps in understanding how they can be used in real applications

It shows an example of how the NFC communication between the Transmitter/Reader works and a generic model of the Transmitter and Receiver

NFC IC block diagram


It provides also a list of existing NFC integrated circuits that include energy harvesting, and the amount of current/volt they can provide to feed sensors.

Commercial NFC ICs with energy harvesting

Then there are some advices about how to design and build a NFC reader and Tag. Particular attention has been dedicated about the NFC Tag

When designing an NFC Tag it is important to consider the environment where the tag will work, the application (as it will limit the area for the antenna) and the desired range/. It is a good idea to cover it with a sheet of ferrite for example if the tag will work close to some metal, as it isolates the traces of metal and so it will work better.

Finally it shows some NFC-based sensors system, and how they can be used for building battery-less seonsors system

Battery-less NFC sensor system

This paper was really helpful because provides information about the NFC ICs with energy harvesting present in the market, a workbench comparing their performance and also some suggested usages. It shows the raising interest in the IoT community about building battery-less sensor systems and how the current technology is able to provide enough energy for powering them.

Wireless charging using NFC technology

This article talks about the applications where NFC can be used for charging devices using WPT and how to increase the efficiency of the power transmission.

The power that can be provided by NFC charging protocol is lower than the one provided by Qi:


NFC Wireless Charging power class vs Qi power class


As the power class are different, the products targeted by these two technologies are not the same.

Then the article shows how to increase the power transfer efficiency.

The first way is to use the same antenna size for both the transmitter and the receiver. As the target are wereables, the size of the antenna must be small.

Impact of the antennas size on efficiency

The space between the transmitter antenna and the receiver antenna also has an impact of the efficiency.
